Calc I Mean Value Theorem

Example Driving across Wyoming

Consider driving across Wyoming on Interstate 80, about 420 miles long. Let's assume the speed limit on I-80 is 70 mph. If it takes more than 6 hrs to get across, what can you say about your speed? What assumptions are you making?


-

If we assume continuous driving and no stops/going backwards, the average speed is less than 70 mph.


Let's go the other way: assume we drive less than 70 mph without any stops. Then the trip will take longer than 6 hours.


+

How is this related to the idea of the derivative? The position function tells us our position along I-80. The derivative tells us our velocity (positive -> going forward, negative -> going backward). Speed is the magnitude of velocity.

We can put this into mathematical notation.

\(f(t) \equiv\) position. \(f'(t) \equiv\) velocity. If \(f'(t) \leq 70\) then \(\displaystyle \frac{f(t_{stop}) - f(t_{start})}{t_{stop} - t_{start}} \leq 70\), or equivalently \(\displaystyle f(t_{stop}) - f(t_{start}) \leq 70 (t_{stop} - t_{start})\),

\(\Rightarrow t_{stop}-t_{start} \geq \frac{f(t_{stop}) - f(t_{start})}{70} = \frac{420}{70} = 6\).




Theorem (Speed limit law): Let \(f\) be a function defined for \(x \in [a,b]\) and let \(M\) be any real number. If \(f'(x) \leq M\) for all \(x \in [a,b]\) then \(f(b) - f(a) \leq M (b-a)\).

Why is this called the speed limit law?

Remark: a similar theorem is true using the \( \geq \).

Theorem - Rolle's Theorem



Theorem (Rolle's Theorem) Suppose \(b > a\) and \(f\) is a continuous function on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\), and that \(f(a) = f(b)\). Then there exists a number \(c\) such that \( f'(c) = 0 \).

Remark: "If the graph goes up and comes back down (and derivatives exist everywhere), then there must be a spot where the derivative is 0."

There are two big assumptions in this theorem: 1) that \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), 2) \(f\) is differentiable. Can you come up with examples that show why these two assumptions are needed to get the consequence of the theorem?

Example -

Consider the function \(f(x) = x^3 - x\) on the interval \([0,1]\). Note that \(f\) is a polynomial, so it is both continuous and differentiable everywhere. Also note that \(f(0) = f(1) = 0\). By Rolle's Theorem we know there is a number \(c \in (0,1)\) such that \(f'(c) = 0\). Find this number.

Theorem - Mean Value Theorem



Theorem (Mean Value Theorem) Suppose \(b > a\) and \(f\) is a continuous function on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\). Then there exists a number \(c\) such that \(f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a}\).

Remark: "There must be a spot where the instantaneous rate of change (the derivative) equals the average rate of change over the interval."

Remark: Rolle's Theorem is just a special case of the Mean Value Theorem. In 'math speak' we say the Mean Value Theorem generalizes Rolle's Theorem. So why study Rolle's theorem at all if the Mean Value Theorem 'is better?' We use the Mean Value Theorem in the special case where Rolle's Theorem applies a lot in proving more advanced theorems in future courses. It is important to know both.

Multiple Choice Questions -

  1. Two racers start a race at the same moment and finish in a tie. Which of the following must be true?
    1. At some point during the race the two racers were not tied.
    2. The racers’ speeds at the end of the race must have been exactly the same.
    3. The racers must have had the same speed at exactly the same time at some point in the race.
    4. The racers had to have the same speed at some moment, but not necessarily at exactly the same time



  2. A function intersects the x-axis at points a and b, where a < b. The slope of the function at a is positive and the slope at b is negative. Which of the following is true for any such function? There exists some point on the interval (a, b) where
    1. the slope is zero and the function has a local maximum.
    2. the slope is zero but there is not a local maximum.
    3. there is a local maximum, but there doesn’t have to be a point at which the slope is zero.
    4. none of the above have to be true.

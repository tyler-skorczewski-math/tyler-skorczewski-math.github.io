Functions of two or three variables

In this lesson you will use sage and ideas about contours and traces to visualize functions of several variables.

  1. The set of points whose distance from the z-axis equals the distance from the xy-plane describes a
    1. Plane
    2. Cylinder
    3. Sphere
    4. Cone
  2. Consider the function \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2\).
    1. What is the domain and range of \(f\)?
    2. What does a trace with constant \(y\) look like?
    3. What does a trace with constant \(x\) look like?
    4. What does a contour with constant \(z\) look like?
    5. Can you put these together to make a sketch of the surface \(z=f(x,y)\)? Check your sketch with the applet above.
  3. Consider the function \(f(x,y) = \sqrt{3x + 4x -12}\). Find and sketch the domain of this function.
  4. Consider the function \(f(x,y) = ye^x\).
    1. What is the domain and range of \(f\)?
    2. What does a trace with constant \(y\) look like?
    3. What does a trace with constant \(x\) look like?
    4. Use the applet above to make a graph of the function. Does the surface agree with your traces?
  5. Consider the function \(f(x,y) = x^2 - y\).
    1. What is the domain of \(f\)?
    2. What does a trace with constant \(y\) look like?
    3. What does a trace with constant \(x\) look like?
    4. What does a contour with constant \(z\) look like?
    5. Can you put these together to make a sketch of the surface \(z=f(x,y)\)? Check your sketch with the applet above.
  6. Consider the function \(w=f(x,y,z) = x^2 + 3y^2 + 5z^2\). Sketch the level sets of this function for \(w=1,5,10\). How would you describe them?
  7. Level surfaces of the function \(f(x, y, z) = \sqrt{x^2 + x^2}\) are
    1. Circles centered at the origin
    2. Spheres centered at the origin
    3. Cylinders centered around the z-axis
    4. Upper-hemispheres centered at the origin
  8. Consider the function \(w=f(x,y,z) = \sqrt{z - x^2 - y^2} \). Describe the domain and level sets for this function.
  9. Suppose on a particular day the fraction of students with the flu is \(r, (0 \le r \le 1)\). If you have \(n\) random encounters with students during the day, the probability of interacting with at least one student with the flu is \(P = 1 - (1-r)^n\).

Fundamental Theorems of Calculus


  1. \( \displaystyle \int_a^b f'(x)~dx = f(b)- f(a) \)

  2. \( \displaystyle \int_C \nabla f \cdot d\vec{r} = f(Q) - f(P) \) where \(C\) begins at \(P\) and ends at \(Q\)

  3. \( \displaystyle \iint_R \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}~dA = \oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} \) where \(\vec{F} = F_1 \hat{x} + F_2 \hat{y}\)

  4. (Stoke's Theorem) Let \(S\) be a bounded, piecewise smooth oriented surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Suppose that \(\partial S\) consists of finitely many piecewise differentiable, simple, closed curves each of which is oriented consistently with \(S\). Let \(\vec{F}\) be continously differentiable vector field whose domain includes \(S\). Then \(\displaystyle \iint_S \nabla \times \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S} = \oint_{\partial S} \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}\).

  5. (Divergence Theorem or Gauss's Theorem) Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded solid region in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) whose boundary \(\partial \Omega\) consists of finitely many piecewise smooth, closed, oriented surfaces each of which is oriented by normals that point away from \(\Omega\). Let \(\vec{F}\) be continously differentiable vector field whose domain includes \(\Omega\). Then \(\displaystyle \oint \hspace{-4.mm}\int_{\partial \Omega} \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S} = \iiint_{\Omega} \nabla \cdot \vec{F}~dV\).

Example - Using Stoke's Theorem

Let \(S\) be the paraboloid \(z = 9 - x^2 - y^2\) defined above the disk in the \(xy\) plane with radius 3. Then \(\partial S\) is the circle \(C = \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid x^2 + y^2 = 9, z = 0\). Let \(\vec{F}= 2z - y \hat{x} + x+z \hat{y} + 3x - 2y \hat{z}\). Show Stoke's Theorem holds by evaulting each side of the equation \(\displaystyle \oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S \nabla \times \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S}\).

Example - More Stoke's Theorem

Let \(S\) be the surface \(z = e^{-x^2 - y^2}\) for \(z \geq 1/e\). Let \(\vec{F}= (e^{y+z} - 2y) \hat{x} + (xe^{y+z} + y) \hat{y} + e^{x+y} \hat{z}\). Calculate \(\displaystyle \iint_S \nabla \times \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S}\).

Example - Applying Divergence Theorem

Let \( \vec{F} = \vec{r} \) and let \(\Omega\) be the cylinder with radius \(a\) and height \(b\) centered at the origin with one base on the \(xy\) plane extending up. Calculate \( \oint \hspace{-2.5mm}\int_{\partial \Omega} \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S}\).

Example - More Divergence Theorem

Let \( \vec{F} = (e^y \cos(z)) \hat{x} + \sqrt{x^3 + 1} \sin(z) \hat{y} + (x^2 + y^2 + 3)\hat{z} \) and let \(S\) be the bell-shaped surface \(z = (1-x^2 -y^2)e^{1-x^2-3y^2}\) for \(z \geq 0\). Calculate \( \iint_{S} \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S}\).

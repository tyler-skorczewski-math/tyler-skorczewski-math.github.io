Calc I U-Substitution

Motivation

Find \(\displaystyle \int \frac{3}{1+9x^2}dx\).

Idea

The idea behind u-substitution is this is the chain rule for derivatives in reverse. Let \(F' = f\). Then \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}( F (u(x))) = \frac{dF}{du}{du}{dx} = F'(u) u'(x) = f(u)u'(x)\). This says that \(F(u(x))\) is an antiderivative of \(f(u)u'(x)\).

We need to recognize \(f(u(x))\) and \(u'(x)\) in the integrand, i.e. we need to find an 'inside' function in the integrand and its derivative. This requires practice and familiarity with derivatives. The best way to get this familiarity is practice.

Example

Find \(\displaystyle \int 3x^2 \cos(x^3)dx\).

Examples - Definitite Integrals

U-substitution works for definite integrals too. We can deal with the limits of the definite integral two ways.

  1. Compute the associated indefinite integral using a u-sub, expressing the antiderivative in terms of the original variable, then doing the evaluations at the limits.
  2. Do a u-sub to transform the definite integral, but also change the limits when doing the u-sub into the u-variable. Then proceed with the new definite integral.

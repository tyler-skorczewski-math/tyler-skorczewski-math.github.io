Theory

Theorem Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix. Let \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, ... , \lambda_n\) be the distinct eigenvalues of \(A\) associated with eigenvectors \(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, ... , \vec{v}_n\). Then the set of eigenvectors are linearly independent.

Application - Isle Royale


Consider Isle Royale in Lake Superior. On Isle Royale there are wolf and moose populations. This nearly closed system provides an ideal setting for studying the ecology between predator and prey species. Let \(w_n\) be the wolf population in year \(n\) and \(m_n\) be the moose population in year \(n\). Assume the population dynamics behave according to the equations below. $$ w_{n+1} = 0.72 w_n + 0.24 m_n$$ $$ m_{n+1} = -0.16w_n + 1.28 m_n$$

  1. Explain the dynamics using common language.
  2. Write the system in matrix vector form \(A\vec{x} = \vec{b}\).
  3. The eigenvalues of this matrix are 1.2 and 0.8. What are the eigenvectors of the matrix \(A\)?
  4. What does the theorem above mean for our problem? How can we use the theorem to make calculating the populations easier?
  5. Assume the population in year 0 is \(\begin{bmatrix}130\\210\end{bmatrix}\). Write this as a linear combination of the eigenvectors.
  6. What is the population in year 3?
  7. What happens as \(n \to \infty\)? Are these populations stable?

Application - Rabbits


Consider a population of rabbits. Half of the rabbits survive their first year of life. Of those that survive, half survive their second year. The max lifespan is 3 years. During the first year these rabbits do not reproduce. During the second year the average number of offspring is 6 rabbits. During the third year the average number of offspring is 8.

  1. Let \(\vec{x}_n = \)[number less than 1 yr old, number between 1 and 2 years old, number between 2 and 3 years old]\(^T\) in year \(n\). Find a matrix \(A\) such that \(\vec{x}_{n+1} = A\vec{x}_n\). The matrix \(A\) is called a Leslie matrix.
  2. What are the eigenvalues of \(A\)?
  3. Is this population stable? Why or why not?

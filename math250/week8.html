Example 3 - Euler Equations


A second order Euler Equation is one of the form $$a x^2 y'' + bxy' + cy = 0$$ where \(a,b,c\) are constants.

  1. Label this DE.
  2. Show that if \(x > 0\), then the substitution \(v = \ln x\) transforms the DE into the constant coefficient linear equation \(a \frac{d^2 y}{dv^2} + (b-a) \frac{dy}{dv} + cy = 0\) with independent variable \(v\).
  3. Label the transformed DE.
  4. If the roots \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) of the characteristic equation are real and distinct, conclude that a general solution of the Euler equation is \(y(x) = c_1 x^{r_1} + c_2 x^{r_2}\).
  5. USe these ideas to solve \(x^2 y'' + xy' - y = 0\).

Example 6 - Operator notation

Let \(D = \frac{d}{dt} \) so that \(Dy = \frac{dy}{dt}\). Note that \(D\) makes no sense unless it can act on something to the right of it. In this way it is analogous to matrix multiplication in that being on the left or right matters. Otherwise we can treat \(D\) somewhat like we treat any other quantity.

  1. Write out the DE represented by \( (D - a) y=0\) for some constant \(a\).
  2. What is its solution?
  3. Write out the DE given by \((D-a)(D-b)y=0\). What is the characteristic equation? What is the general solution?
  4. What is the DE and general solution for \( (D-a)^2 y = 0\)?
  5. Take notes on why these ideas can be used to build the general solutions to DEs where the characteristic equation has repeated roots.

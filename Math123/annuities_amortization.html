Annuities & Amortization

Formulas

  • Annuities
    • future value: \(\displaystyle FV = R \frac{(1+i)^n - 1}{i} \)

    • present value: \(\displaystyle P = R \left[ \frac{1 - (1 + i)^n}{i}\right]\)
  • Amortization
    • payments: \(\displaystyle R = P \frac{(i}{1 - (1+i)^{-n}} \)

    • loan balance: \(\displaystyle Balance = P (1 + i)^n - R \left[ \frac{(1 + i)^n - 1}{i} \right] \)
  • Question: What does each variable represent in the equations above?

Example - Paying Points?

When taking out a mortgage a point is simply 1% of the loan amount. In the mortgage process, a bank typically offers people several loan options. These loan options will have different interest rates but in order to get a lower rate, one must pay the bank by paying points. In a way this can be thought of a prepaying interest to the bank. For example, let’s look at a couple buying a house for $150,000. Let’s assume they put 20% down, so the loan amount is $120,000. We will neglect any closing costs associated with the mortgage. A bank may offer these people a 30 year loan option with a 4% annual interest rate compounded monthly and another option with a 3.75% interest rate if they pay 1 point at the beginning of the loan. Which option is better for this couple? It depends on how many months they plan on staying in the house. (Many people do not stay in a home for the entire length of their mortgage.) If they live there a short time they may not recoup the money they paid upfront in lower monthly payments. Determine the number of months the couple must stay in the house for paying points to be worth it.
